# Bluest Hour Source Sheet

This file gathers the source directions, Consensus searches, and candidate papers connected to the Bluest Hour research question.

Use it as:

- a teacher-facing reference during class
- a student-facing model of how a project question connects to real research
- a bridge between `research-journal.md`, `PAPER.md`, and the Session 7 source-search work

---

## The core research question

The Bluest Hour question is:

> When people write short reflections after blue-hour-timed walks, does a generic positive/negative sentiment model actually capture their mood, or does it flatten a more mixed reflective style of writing?

This is not one giant question. It breaks into three smaller research buckets:

1. **Nature / daylight / mood**
2. **Reflective writing / journaling**
3. **Sentiment analysis / model fit**

That is the key move students can copy for their own papers.

---

## Bucket 1 — Nature / daylight / mood

### What this bucket helps explain

This bucket supports the claim that the walk itself may matter as a mood or wellbeing variable, not just as a decorative setting for the app.

### Exact Consensus searches

- `nature exposure mood ecological momentary assessment`
- `daylight exposure affect ecological momentary assessment`
- `outdoor light exposure mood study`

### Candidate sources

#### Beute & de Kort (2018)

**The natural context of wellbeing: Ecological momentary assessment of the influence of nature and daylight on affect and stress**

Why it helps:
- shows that everyday exposure to nature and daylight can affect affect and stress
- gives the Bluest Hour project a real research neighborhood in momentary mood measurement

#### Shankman et al. (2025)

Daylight exposure improving mood independent of physical activity

Why it helps:
- supports the idea that light itself may matter, not just the walk as exercise
- helps separate “this feels poetic” from “this belongs to a real wellbeing question”

#### Paredes-Céspedes et al. (2024)

**The Effects of Nature Exposure Therapies on Stress, Depression, and Anxiety Levels: A Systematic Review**

Why it helps:
- gives broad support that nature exposure belongs to a real mental-health / wellbeing literature
- useful when explaining why the project’s setting is not arbitrary

## Bucket 2 — Reflective writing / journaling

### What this bucket helps explain

This bucket supports the idea that collecting a short field note is a meaningful method, not just an aesthetic flourish.

### Exact Consensus searches

- `reflective writing well-being review`
- `journaling mood measurement study`
- `reflective writing meta synthesis`

### Candidate sources

#### Artioli et al. (2021)

**Health professionals and students’ experiences of reflective writing in learning: A qualitative meta-synthesis**

Why it helps:
- supports reflective writing as a meaningful practice and method
- even though the domain is different, it helps explain why a reflective note is not random busywork

#### General source direction

Studies on journaling as mood measurement or reflective writing and wellbeing

Why it helps:
- gives support for using short written reflections as evidence
- helps students see that the “field note” can count as data, even at a small scale

## Bucket 3 — Sentiment analysis / model fit

### What this bucket helps explain

This bucket supports the claim that the binary sentiment baseline may be a poor fit for reflective, mixed, or diary-like writing.

### Exact Consensus searches

- `sentiment analysis diary text`
- `emotion classification reflective writing`
- `sentiment analysis health and well-being systematic review`

### Candidate sources

#### Zunic, Corcoran, & Spasic (2020)

**Sentiment Analysis in Health and Well-Being: Systematic Review**

Why it helps:
- shows that sentiment analysis in wellbeing-related text is a real research area
- also supports the idea that domain fit is hard and that text type matters

#### General source direction

Diary text, reflective writing, or wellbeing text classification

Why it helps:
- supports the argument that a generic positive/negative model may flatten subtle writing
- gives a research frame for the Bluest Hour “baseline revealed a better question” claim

## Why a personal, idiosyncratic app can still be research

The Bluest Hour app is personal and local, but it still works as a research project because it does more than express taste.

It does three research-like things:

1. **It creates a testable question.**
   The app is not only “a beautiful thing.” It creates a question about timing, mood, reflective writing, and model fit.

2. **It produces evidence.**
   The walk timing, the local offset, the journal entries, and the model outputs are all things you can compare and reason about.

3. **It reveals a mismatch.**
   The most important research move in Bluest Hour is that the simple sentiment model does not seem to fit the kind of writing the app creates. That mismatch becomes the research question.

So the project counts as research not because it is universal or impersonal, but because it moves from:

**personal observation → testable question → small body of evidence → honest limitation**

That is enough to be a real student research project.
